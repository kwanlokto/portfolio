# Why *The Pragmatic Programmer* Still Matters

**Andrew Hunt & David Thomas (1999, 20th Anniversary Edition 2019)**

---

I picked up *The Pragmatic Programmer* expecting dated advice. What I found was more valuable — and more frustrating.

Most technical books teach you *what* to do. This one teaches you *how to think*. Written before Git, before cloud computing, before half the tools we take for granted, it doesn't prescribe solutions — it prescribes a mindset: systems change, requirements evolve, and your job is to build software that survives both.

**The ideas that stuck:** DRY isn't just "don't copy-paste" — it's recognizing when the same business rule lives in three places that should be one. The "broken windows" theory is uncomfortably accurate; neglect is contagious, and "we'll clean it up later" has a way of becoming permanent. Tool mastery matters more than it sounds — small inefficiencies compound over years into weeks of lost time.

**The core insight that aged perfectly:** *"There are no final decisions."* Every system will face requirements you didn't anticipate. The question isn't whether your code will change — it's whether it can change without breaking.

**The uncomfortable truth:** The book presents an idealized world where teams have time to refactor and technical debt doesn't accrue faster than you can pay it down. It raises the hard questions — what do you do when "broken windows" *is* the company culture? — without fully answering them. Maybe it can't.

Read it. Come back in a few years and see what lands differently.

**Rating: 4.75/5**
